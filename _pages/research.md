We study the evolution of plant-insect interactions from both ecological and molecular/genetic perspectives. Our primary study systems are interactions between native Solanaceous plants (e.g. Datura spp., wild tobaccos) and their specialist herbivores in the Southwestern United States; however, we have many ongoing projects with other systems as well. See below for details about some of our ongoing research projects.

Searching for genomic signatures of coevolution
  Coevolution - when interacting species exert selective pressures on one antother in a reciprocal manner - is often assumed to be governing the evolution of interaction-mediating traits; however, recent work in a variety of systems have shown that reciprocal adaptation between interacting species is not universal. Natural selection often operates uni-directionally, with only one species adapting to interactions with another. Some species interactions may occur through chance alone, with no interaction-derived natural selection acting on any species involved. We use a variety of approachs - phylogenomics, population genetics, and transcriptomics - to tease apart the evolutionary dynamics of species interactions.

Evolution of inter-kingdom molecular interactions
  Herbivorous insects, like many parasites and pathogens, are capable of manipulating their hosts to evade defense/immune responses. This is accomplished with a suite of highly specialized proteins - known as effectors - in their saliva. Salivary effectors enter host-plant cells during feeding and alter the plant's physiology to benefit herbivores by interacting with key signalling molecules that mediate the perception of herbivores and mounting of defensive responses. These inter-kingdom molecular interactions have been well-described in some hemipteran pests, such as aphids, providing an opportunity to explore the evolutionary processes that generated and maintain them.
